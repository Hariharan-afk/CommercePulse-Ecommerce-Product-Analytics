# CommercePulse Final Business Insights

## Executive Summary

CommercePulse analyzes October–November 2019 e-commerce behavior across **109M+ events** to understand why November revenue and cart activity increased while cart-to-purchase conversion declined.

The project found that November growth was commercially strong but operationally mixed:

- Revenue increased from **$229.96M** to **$275.19M**.
- Sessions increased from **9.24M** to **13.78M**.
- Active users increased from **3.02M** to **3.70M**.
- View-to-cart rate increased from **6.18%** to **12.57%**.
- Cart-to-purchase rate declined from **49.17%** to **37.10%**.

The key conclusion:

> November brought more shopping activity and higher revenue, but downstream conversion quality weakened after users reached the cart stage.

---

## Core Business Question

The project investigated:

> Why did November revenue and cart activity increase while cart-to-purchase conversion declined?

To answer this, the analysis moved through six diagnostic levels:

```text
Platform → Category → Subcategory → Brand → Price Band → Brand × Price Band
```

---

## Insight 1 — Platform Growth Was Strong, But Funnel Quality Weakened

At the platform level, November showed strong growth across activity and revenue metrics.

| Metric | October 2019 | November 2019 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Total Events | 42.45M | 67.50M | Increased |
| Active Users | 3.02M | 3.70M | Increased |
| Sessions | 9.24M | 13.78M | Increased |
| Purchase Events | 742.85K | 916.94K | Increased |
| Purchase Revenue | $229.96M | $275.19M | +19.67% |
| View → Cart Rate | 6.18% | 12.57% | +6.39 pp |
| Cart → Purchase Rate | 49.17% | 37.10% | -12.07 pp |

### Interpretation

November did not suffer from a lack of traffic or purchase demand. Instead, the issue appeared downstream: more users added products to carts, but a smaller share of cart sessions converted into purchases.

---

## Insight 2 — Revenue Growth Was Concentrated in a Few Categories

Revenue growth was not broad-based across the entire catalog. A small number of categories contributed most positive growth.

The most important category-level contributors were:

- Electronics
- Unknown / uncategorized
- Appliances
- Computers

Electronics was the dominant category growth driver.

| Category | Revenue Delta | Cart → Purchase Change |
|---|---:|---:|
| Electronics | +$28.79M | -11.35 pp |
| Unknown | +$6.96M | Negative |
| Appliances | +$5.06M | Negative |
| Computers | +$2.62M | Negative |

### Interpretation

Electronics was both the largest revenue growth driver and a major conversion-risk category. This made electronics the highest-priority area for deeper investigation.

---

## Insight 3 — Electronics Was the Main Growth Engine and Conversion-Risk Category

Electronics generated the largest November revenue increase, but also showed a major deterioration in cart-to-purchase efficiency.

| Electronics Metric | Value |
|---|---:|
| November Revenue | $205.25M |
| Revenue Delta | +$28.79M |
| Cart → Purchase Change | -11.35 pp |

### Interpretation

Electronics was not simply growing. It was growing while becoming less efficient at converting cart sessions into purchases. This suggested the need to drill into electronics subcategories.

---

## Insight 4 — Smartphones Dominated Electronics

Within electronics, smartphones explained the majority of revenue and growth.

| Smartphone Metric | Value |
|---|---:|
| November Revenue | $177.82M |
| Share of Electronics Revenue | 86.64% |
| Revenue Delta | +$20.77M |
| Cart → Purchase Change | -11.65 pp |

### Interpretation

The electronics story was primarily a smartphone story. Smartphones were the most important electronics subcategory because they combined:

- dominant revenue scale,
- large revenue growth,
- and major cart-to-purchase deterioration.

---

## Insight 5 — Apple and Samsung Drove Most Smartphone Revenue

At the brand level, smartphone revenue was highly concentrated in Apple and Samsung.

| Brand | November Revenue | Revenue Share | Revenue Delta | Cart → Purchase Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Apple | $115.26M | 64.82% | +$12.71M | -12.07 pp |
| Samsung | $42.71M | 24.02% | +$5.34M | -10.97 pp |
| Xiaomi | $9.84M | 5.54% | Positive | Negative |

Apple and Samsung together represented the majority of smartphone revenue and growth.

### Interpretation

Apple was the highest-priority brand because it had:

- the largest revenue base,
- the largest revenue increase,
- the highest smartphone revenue share,
- and a major cart-to-purchase decline.

Samsung was the second most important brand, with strong growth but also weaker downstream conversion.

---

## Insight 6 — Premium Smartphone Tiers Drove Much of November Growth

Smartphone growth was strongly influenced by premium price tiers.

| Price Band | November Revenue | Revenue Delta | Growth Share | Cart → Purchase Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| $800–$1199 | $50.91M | +$9.15M | 44.06% | -13.53 pp |
| $1200+ | $39.80M | +$5.90M | 28.38% | Negative |
| $100–$249 | $30.14M | +$6.29M | 30.29% | Negative |

The **$800–$1199** price band was the most important price-tier segment because it had the largest revenue delta and a major cart-to-purchase decline.

### Interpretation

November smartphone growth was not only premium-led. The $100–$249 value segment also expanded meaningfully. However, the strongest commercial signal came from premium smartphone tiers.

---

## Insight 7 — Brand × Price-Band Analysis Revealed the Most Actionable Segments

The final diagnostic layer combined brand and price band to identify exact growth and risk pockets.

### Top Growth Segments

| Segment | Revenue Delta | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Apple $800–$1199 | +$7.70M | Largest smartphone growth contributor |
| Apple $1200+ | +$6.10M | Major premium growth contributor |
| Samsung $100–$249 | +$4.71M | Major value-tier growth pocket |

### Underperformance Segment

| Segment | Signal |
|---|---|
| Samsung $250–$499 | Revenue decline and sharp cart-to-purchase deterioration |

### Interpretation

The smartphone growth story was concentrated in a few brand-price combinations. Apple premium tiers explained much of the premium growth, while Samsung showed a split pattern: strong value-tier growth but mid-range underperformance.

## Business Interpretation

November growth was commercially positive but operationally mixed.

The platform successfully generated:

- more sessions,
- more active users,
- more purchases,
- more revenue,
- and more carting behavior.

However, the increase in carting did not translate proportionally into purchases.

The most important conversion risk was concentrated in commercially valuable smartphone segments, especially:

- Apple premium tiers,
- Samsung value-tier growth,
- Samsung mid-range underperformance,
- and premium smartphone cart abandonment.

---

## Business Recommendation

The business should prioritize deeper investigation into high-value smartphone funnel behavior.

### Priority 1 — Apple Premium Tiers

Investigate:

- Apple $800–$1199 sessions
- Apple $1200+ sessions
- premium smartphone cart abandonment
- price sensitivity
- product availability
- checkout friction

Reason:

> Apple premium tiers delivered major revenue growth but also showed meaningful downstream conversion deterioration.

---

### Priority 2 — Samsung Value-Tier Growth

Investigate:

- Samsung $100–$249 users
- product-level growth drivers
- whether value-tier growth came from new users or repeat users
- whether this segment can be expanded profitably

Reason:

> Samsung $100–$249 created a meaningful value-tier growth pocket.

---

### Priority 3 — Samsung Mid-Range Underperformance

Investigate:

- Samsung $250–$499 products
- price competitiveness
- product assortment
- inventory availability
- cart abandonment reasons

Reason:

> Samsung $250–$499 stood out as a weak segment because it showed both revenue decline and sharp cart-to-purchase deterioration.

---

### Priority 4 — Cart-to-Purchase Friction

Investigate:

- checkout drop-off
- shipping costs
- payment issues
- product availability
- promotion expiration
- delivery estimates
- user comparison-shopping behavior

Reason:

> Users were adding more products to cart, but a smaller share of cart sessions converted into purchases.

---

## Suggested Next Analyses

The next analytical steps should combine this dashboard with additional data sources:

### 1. Product-Level Analysis

Identify specific products responsible for:

- highest revenue growth,
- largest cart abandonment,
- largest purchase decline,
- and largest conversion deterioration.

### 2. User Cohort Analysis

Analyze whether November growth came from:

- new users,
- returning users,
- high-intent users,
- one-time buyers,
- or repeat purchasers.

### 3. Promotion Analysis

Determine whether November promotions increased carting behavior without producing proportional purchase conversion.

### 4. Inventory and Availability Analysis

Investigate whether high-value smartphone products suffered from:

- stockouts,
- limited availability,
- delayed delivery,
- or price changes.

### 5. Checkout Funnel Analysis

Analyze whether users dropped off due to:

- payment issues,
- shipping fees,
- tax surprises,
- account friction,
- or delivery constraints.

---

## Final Recommendation

The business should treat November as a successful growth month but a warning signal for funnel quality.

The highest-priority recommendation is:

> Investigate premium smartphone cart abandonment, especially Apple $800–$1199 and Apple $1200+ sessions, while separately diagnosing Samsung $250–$499 underperformance.

This recommendation is supported by the fact that these segments combine high revenue impact, strong growth or decline signals, and meaningful cart-to-purchase deterioration.
